## 1. DOW JONES INDUSTRIAL AVERAGE — 100-YEAR HISTORY

### What Is the DJIA
- Price-weighted index of 30 large US companies
- Created by Charles Dow and Edward Jones, first published May 26, 1896

- Created by Standard & Poor's, launched in its current form March 4, 1957
- Predecessor: S&P 90 (1926)

- Rebalanced quarterly; membership reviewed quarterly
- Free-float market cap weighted

## 3. GREAT DEPRESSION 1929–1933

### Timeline
- **August 1929:** Business activity peaks (NBER start of recession)
- **September 3, 1929:** DJIA peak at 381.17
- **October 24, 1929 (Black Thursday):** First major crash day; DJIA drops 11% intraday; partial recovery
- **October 28, 1929 (Black Monday):** DJIA falls 12.8%
- **October 29, 1929 (Black Tuesday):** DJIA falls 11.7%; volume ~16 million shares (record)
- **Combined Oct 28-29:** −23.6% in two days
- **November 13, 1929:** DJIA at 198.6 (first bottom, −48% from peak in 6 weeks)
- **April 17, 1930:** DJIA recovers to 294 (suckers' rally, +52% from Nov low)
- **July 8, 1932:** DJIA final trough at 41.22 (−89.2% from 1929 peak)
- **NBER Recession end:** March 1933 (43 months)
- **DJIA recovery to 1929 peak:** November 23, 1954 (25 years later)

### Depth and Scale
- DJIA peak to trough: −89.2%
- S&P composite equivalent: −86.2%
- US GDP decline: ~26.7% real GDP from 1929 to 1933
- Unemployment peak: ~24.9% (1933) — roughly 13 million out of work
- Bank failures: ~9,000 banks failed 1930–1933 (roughly 40% of all US banks)
- US money supply (M2) contracted ~33% 1929–1933
- Wholesale prices fell ~32%
- International trade collapsed ~66% globally

### Causes
1. **Overleveraged stock speculation:** Buying stocks on margin (10:1 leverage common), 1920s bubble
2. **Fed policy failure:** Fed raised rates in 1931 to defend gold standard while economy collapsed
3. **Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930):** 50% tariffs on 20,000+ goods → global trade collapse → retaliatory tariffs
4. **Banking collapse:** No deposit insurance (FDIC created 1933), bank runs destroyed savings
5. **Debt deflation spiral:** Asset prices fell → collateral value fell → loans called → fire sales → more deflation
6. **Gold standard straitjacket:** Could not expand money supply to fight deflation
7. **Fiscal austerity:** Hoover initially raised taxes and cut spending (wrong response)

### Policy Response and Recovery
- FDR New Deal: March 1933 – bank holiday, Emergency Banking Act, FDIC
- Gold confiscation order: April 5, 1933 (Executive Order 6102) — ended gold convertibility
- Glass-Steagall Act 1933: Separated commercial and investment banking
- Securities Exchange Act 1934: Created SEC
- GDP recovery: Returned to 1929 levels by ~1936–37 (nominal) but 1937 recession hit again
- Full recovery: WWII spending 1941–1945 finally ended depression

### Lessons
- Central bank inaction in financial panics causes catastrophic outcomes
- Deflation spirals are harder to escape than inflation
- Credit contraction amplifies all economic shocks
- Fiscal austerity in a depression is self-defeating

## 4. BLACK MONDAY — OCTOBER 19, 1987

### What Happened
- **Date:** Monday, October 19, 1987
- **DJIA:** Opened at 2,246.74; closed at 1,738.74
- **Single-day decline: −22.6% (DJIA) / −20.5% (S&P 500)**
- Largest single-day percentage decline in DJIA history
- Previous day (Friday Oct 16): DJIA already fell −4.6%
- Two-day combined: −26%
- Volume: 604 million shares (NYSE) — double previous record
- Global chain: Market fell in Hong Kong (−45%), Australia (−41%), UK (−26%) that same week

### Timeline (Oct–Dec 1987)
- **Aug 25, 1987:** DJIA peaks at 2,722
- **Oct 2, 1987:** DJIA begins pullback
- **Oct 16, 1987 (Friday):** DJIA −4.6% on rising rates fears
- **Oct 19, 1987 (Black Monday):** −22.6%
- **Oct 20, 1987:** Circuit breakers tested; market bounces +5.9%
- **Dec 4, 1987:** DJIA closes at 1,766 (secondary bottom after bounce and re-test)
- **Peak-to-trough:** −36% (Aug to Dec)
- **Recovery to prior peak:** September 1989 (approximately 2 years)

### Causes
1. **Portfolio insurance:** A hedging strategy that automatically sold S&P 500 futures as prices fell — created mechanical selling cascade
2. **Rising interest rates:** 10-year Treasury yield rose from 7.5% to 10.2% in weeks prior
3. **Overvaluation:** P/E ratios at elevated levels (~20x)
4. **Trade deficit concerns:** Large US trade deficit announced October 14
5. **Rising inflation fears:** CPI creeping higher
6. **Program trading:** Computer-driven trading amplified volatility
7. **Global contagion:** Markets worldwide fell that week in synchronized fashion
8. **Lack of circuit breakers:** No mechanism to halt trading during cascade

### Policy Response
- Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan issued statement Oct 20: "The Federal Reserve, consistent with its responsibilities as the Nation's central bank, affirmed today its readiness to serve as a source of liquidity to support the economic and financial system."
- Fed flooded banking system with liquidity
- No recession followed (unlike most market crashes) — unique outcome
- Led directly to NYSE circuit breaker creation (Rule 80B, later updated)

### Legacy and Lessons
- First demonstration of "Greenspan Put" concept — Fed will intervene
- Portfolio insurance strategies blamed and largely abandoned
- Created modern market circuit breakers (now at −7%, −13%, −20% for market-wide halts)
- Proved that crashes can occur without recession following

## 5. DOT-COM CRASH 2000–2002

### The Bubble (1995–2000)
- Internet commercialization began ~1994-1995 (Netscape IPO August 1995)
- NASDAQ: 1,000 in 1995 → 5,048.62 peak on March 10, 2000 (+400% in 5 years)
- S&P 500: 1,000 (1997) → 1,527 peak (March 24, 2000)
- 457 internet IPOs in 1999–2000, many with no revenue
- Valuations based on "eyeballs," "burn rate," "land grab" narratives
- P/E ratio of S&P 500 peaked at ~44x (long-run avg ~15–17x)
- NASDAQ P/E essentially infinite for most components

### Peak Dates
- **NASDAQ peak:** March 10, 2000 at 5,048.62
- **S&P 500 peak:** March 24, 2000 at 1,527.46

### The Crash
- **NASDAQ trough:** October 9, 2002 at 1,114.11 (**−78.0% from peak**)
- **S&P 500 trough:** October 9, 2002 at 776.76 (−49.1% from peak)
- **DJIA trough:** October 9, 2002 at 7,286 (−38% from peak)
- Duration: March 2000 – October 2002 (31 months)
- NBER Recession: March 2001 – November 2001 (8 months)

### What Happened Year by Year
- **2000:** Fed raised rates to 6.5% (May 2000). Dot-coms running out of cash. NASDAQ fell 39% in 2000.
- **2001:** September 11 attacks. Afghan War. NASDAQ fell additional 21%. Many dot-coms bankrupt.
- **2002:** Accounting scandals: Enron (Dec 2001 bankruptcy), WorldCom ($11B fraud, July 2002), Tyco, Adelphia. NASDAQ fell additional 31%.

### Companies That Died (Dot-com Graveyard)
- Pets.com: IPO Feb 2000, bankrupt Nov 2000 (9 months)
- Webvan: $375M raised, bankrupt July 2001
- Kozmo.com: $280M raised, bankrupt April 2001
- Boo.com: $135M raised, bankrupt May 2000
- eToys: Bankrupt March 2001
- Flooz.com: $35M raised, bankrupt August 2001
- drkoop.com: Bankrupt June 2001
- Many telecoms: Global Crossing, MCI WorldCom, 360networks — collective $2+ trillion in market cap destroyed

### Companies That Survived and Thrived
- Amazon (AMZN): Fell from $106 (Dec 1999) to $5.51 (Sep 2001) = −95%. Survived to become ~$2T company.
- Google: IPO August 2004 (after crash), went public at $85, now $100+
- Apple: Survived; Steve Jobs returned 1997; iPod 2001 saved company
- eBay: Survived crash; PayPal IPO 2002
- Cisco: Lost $400B market cap 2000–2002 but survived (hardware, not pure dot-com)

### NASDAQ Recovery
- Return to 5,000 level: April 24, 2015 (15 years after peak)
- Fully surpassed old peak: July 2016

### Causes
1. Federal Reserve kept rates too low in 1998–99 (LTCM/Y2K fears)
2. IPO mania: Investment banks pushed companies to market with no earnings
3. Media hype: CNBC, business press amplified every startup story
4. Venture capital herding: Everyone chasing same "winner-take-all" narratives
5. Accounting opacity: Revenue recognition abuses, barter deals
6. Y2K preparation: Companies overspent on tech in 1999, cut sharply in 2000
7. Insider lock-ups expiring: Founders sold into the peak

### Signals That Appeared Before the Crash
- Fed tightening aggressively (6 hikes 1999–2000)
- Yield curve flattening
- Many IPOs immediately fell below offering price by late 1999
- Margin debt at records ($278B peak March 2000)
- P/E ratios at extreme multiples (20–50x forward, 100–200x trailing for many names)
- Short interest rising on high-profile names

## 6. 2008 GLOBAL FINANCIAL CRISIS

### The Housing Bubble (2003–2006)
- US home prices rose 124% nationally from 1997 to 2006 (Case-Shiller index)
- Subprime mortgage origination: $100B (2001) → $600B (2006)
- Private label securitization: Mortgages bundled into MBS (Mortgage-Backed Securities)
- CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations): Repackaged MBS tranches; AAA-rated by design
- Synthetic CDOs: Bets ON MBS using credit default swaps (no actual mortgages)
- Leverage: Investment banks at 30–40:1 leverage (Bear Stearns, Lehman)
- Housing starts: 2.3 million annually (2006 peak)

### Timeline of Crisis
- **June 2006:** Fed Funds Rate peaks at 5.25%; Case-Shiller home price index peaks
- **Feb 2007:** HSBC first major bank to disclose subprime losses ($10.5B)
- **April 2007:** New Century Financial (major subprime lender) bankrupt
- **Jun 2007:** Two Bear Stearns hedge funds fail (subprime exposure)
- **Aug 2007:** BNP Paribas freezes three funds; interbank lending freezes (LIBOR spike)
- **Sep 2007:** Northern Rock bank run (UK, first since 1866); Fed cuts 50bp emergency
- **Jan 2008:** Citigroup, Merrill Lynch disclose massive write-downs
- **Mar 14, 2008:** Bear Stearns collapses; Fed arranges JPMorgan rescue at $2/share (later $10)
- **July 2008:** IndyMac (9th largest US bank) seized by FDIC
- **Sep 7, 2008:** Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac nationalized ($5.3T in mortgages)
- **Sep 15, 2008:** Lehman Brothers files Chapter 11 (largest bankruptcy in US history: $639B assets)
- **Sep 16, 2008:** AIG rescued by Fed ($85B emergency loan — AIG had $2.7T in CDS exposure)
- **Sep 17, 2008:** Money market funds "break the buck" (Reserve Primary Fund NAV fell to $0.97)
- **Sep 25, 2008:** Washington Mutual seized ($307B assets — largest US bank failure ever)
- **Oct 3, 2008:** TARP signed ($700B bank bailout)
- **Oct 2008:** Market panic deepens; S&P 500 falls 17% in October alone
- **Nov 25, 2008:** QE1 announced (Fed buys $600B MBS)
- **Mar 9, 2009:** S&P 500 trough at 666.79

### Market Decline Statistics
- **S&P 500 peak:** October 9, 2007 at 1,565.15
- **S&P 500 trough:** March 9, 2009 at 666.79
- **Decline: −57.7% (round number cited as −56.8%)**
- **Duration:** 17 months
- **DJIA peak:** 14,164 (Oct 9, 2007)
- **DJIA trough:** 6,547 (Mar 9, 2009) — −53.8%

### Economic Damage
- US GDP contraction: −4.3% peak-to-trough (Q4 2007 to Q2 2009)
- Unemployment peak: 10.0% (October 2009)
- Housing prices fell: ~32% nationally (Case-Shiller), 50%+ in bubble markets (Las Vegas, Miami, Phoenix)
- Household net worth loss: ~$13 trillion
- Bank losses globally: >$2 trillion written off
- 8.7 million jobs lost 2008–2009

### CDO/MBS Architecture (The Fraud)
- Mortgage → Originator (gets fee, sells loan) → Securitizer (Goldman, Lehman, etc.)
- → MBS pool → CDO repackages junior tranches → CDO-Squared → Synthetic CDO (pure bets)
- Rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) gave AAA ratings using flawed models
- Models assumed home prices would never fall nationally simultaneously
- Correlation assumptions were catastrophically wrong
- "Originate to distribute" model removed incentive for loan quality

### Policy Response
- **Federal Reserve:** Cut FFR from 5.25% to 0–0.25% (Dec 2008); QE1, QE2, QE3
- **Treasury:** TARP $700B (actual cost ~$25B after repayments with interest)
- **Congress:** Economic Stimulus Act 2008 ($168B), ARRA 2009 ($831B)
- **FDIC:** Guaranteed bank deposits, expanded limits from $100K to $250K
- **International:** G20 coordinated $5T+ in stimulus globally
- Dodd-Frank Act (2010): Volcker Rule, stress tests, resolution authority

### Recovery
- S&P 500 trough to recovery of 2007 highs: **March 2013 (4 years)**
- S&P 500 trough to doubling: 2013 (4 years, 100% gain)
- US unemployment back to 5%: December 2015 (6+ years)
- Longest bull market in history began March 2009

## 7. COVID CRASH 2020

### The Event
- First US COVID-19 case: January 21, 2020
- WHO declares pandemic: March 11, 2020
- US declares national emergency: March 13, 2020
- First US lockdowns: California, New York, March 19–22, 2020

### Market Timeline
- **S&P 500 pre-COVID peak:** February 19, 2020 at 3,386.15
- **S&P 500 trough:** March 23, 2020 at 2,237.40
- **Decline: −33.9%**
- **Duration of crash: 33 calendar days (fastest bear market in history)**
- DJIA fell from 29,551 (Feb 12) to 18,591 (Mar 23) = −37.1%
- 10 trading days from Feb 19 to -20% bear market threshold (fastest ever)
- Circuit breakers triggered 4 times in 2 weeks

### Panic Phase (Feb 20 – Mar 23)
- Oil price war: Saudi Arabia vs Russia (March 8) — Brent crude from $50 to $20
- Credit markets froze: HY spreads spiked to 1,100bp (worst since 2009)
- Investment grade spreads: 374bp (record outside GFC)
- Treasury market dysfunction: Even 10-year Treasuries had bid-ask spreads blow out
- Fed response (March 3): Emergency 50bp cut (not scheduled FOMC)
- Fed response (March 15, Sunday): Emergency 100bp cut to 0; QE $700B announced
- March 23: Fed announced unlimited QE; corporate bond buying program (unprecedented)

### The Recovery — Fastest Ever
- **V-shaped recovery:** 6 months to recover all losses (August 18, 2020)
- Previous fastest recovery: ~3 months (1987 Black Monday — but far smaller decline)
- S&P 500 at year-end 2020: +16.3% for full year (crash AND recovery in same calendar year)
- NASDAQ 2020 full year: +43.6%

### Why Recovery Was So Fast
1. **Fiscal stimulus speed and scale:**
   - CARES Act (March 27, 2020): $2.2 trillion ($1,200 checks, $600/week unemployment, $669B PPP)
   - Additional packages: $900B (Dec 2020), $1.9T ARP (March 2021)
   - Total direct US fiscal response: ~$5.5–6 trillion
2. **Fed actions — unprecedented breadth:**
   - FFR to 0–0.25%
   - QE "unlimited" (eventually purchased $4.6T)
   - Corporate bond buying (Investment Grade first, then HY via ETFs)
   - Main Street Lending Program
   - Municipal Lending Facility
   - Money Market Mutual Fund Facility
3. **Vaccine development speed:** Pfizer/BioNTech Dec 11, 2020 FDA emergency use (11 months — record)
4. **Digital economy acceleration:** Work-from-home, e-commerce, cloud computing — sectors gained
5. **Pent-up demand release:** Consumers saved $2.5T+ in forced lockdown savings

### Winners of COVID Crash
- Zoom (ZM): $68 pre-COVID → $559 peak (Oct 2020) = +722%
- Peloton (PTON): $20 → $171 = +755%
- Moderna (MRNA): $18 → $497 = +2,661%
- Tesla (TSLA): $83 → $1,243 = +1,397% (2020 full year)
- Amazon: +76% in 2020
- Netflix: +67% in 2020

### Losers During COVID
- Airlines: United, Delta, American −50 to −70% (recovered over 2021–2022)
- Hotels/Cruise: Marriott, Carnival −50 to −80%
- Energy: XLE ETF −32% in 2020 (oil briefly negative April 20, 2020: −$37/barrel)
- Retail (brick-and-mortar): Macy's, JC Penney, Neiman Marcus (bankrupt)

## 9. ADDITIONAL KEY MARKET EVENTS

### 1997 Asian Financial Crisis
- Thai baht collapse July 2, 1997 → contagion to Indonesia, Korea, Philippines, Malaysia
- DJIA correction: ~15% (July–October 1997)
- IMF bailouts: ~$110 billion combined
- S&P 500 barely affected — US decoupled from EM crisis

### 1998 LTCM / Russia Default Crisis
- Russia defaulted on domestic debt August 17, 1998
- Long-Term Capital Management (Nobel Prize economists) lost $4.6B in weeks
- LTCM had $1.25 trillion in derivative positions on $5B equity (250:1 leverage)
- S&P 500: −19.3% from peak (July–October 1998)
- Fed organized 14-bank bailout to prevent systemic collapse
- Fed cut rates 75bp (3 cuts) September–November 1998
- Market recovered fully by year-end 1998

### 2010–2012 European Sovereign Debt Crisis
- Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, Italy — sovereign debt spiraling
- PIIGS crisis: Bond yields on Greek debt peaked at 37%+
- S&P 500 correction: ~19% (April–October 2011)
- ECB "whatever it takes" speech (Draghi, July 26, 2012) ended crisis
- Key lesson: Policy credibility can substitute for full monetization

### 2015–2016 China Crash / Oil Collapse
- Chinese stock market fell 40% (June–August 2015) after China's circuit breaker malfunction
- Oil fell from $110 (2014) to $27 (Jan 2016) — 75% decline
- S&P 500: −14% (August 2015), then −15% (early 2016)
- HY spreads widened to 850bp (energy sector spreads >1,000bp)
- US energy companies: 130+ oil and gas bankruptcies 2015–2017

### 2018 Q4 Selloff
- S&P 500: −19.8% October–December 2018 (near bear market)
- Cause: Fed tightening fears (Powell's "long way from neutral" comment), trade war, slowing growth
- Christmas Eve 2018: S&P fell to 2,351 (low for year)
- Recovery: Full by April 2019
- Fed pivoted: "Patient" language introduced Jan 2019; cuts began July 2019
