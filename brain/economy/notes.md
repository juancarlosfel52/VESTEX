## 1. INFLATION

### What Is Inflation
Inflation is the sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services over time. It erodes purchasing power: $1 in 1913 is worth roughly $0.03 today.

### Key Measures

#### CPI — Consumer Price Index
- Published by: Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), monthly
- Series ID: CPIAUCSL (all items, seasonally adjusted), CPIAUCNS (not seasonally adjusted)
- Basket: ~94,000 goods/services, 8 major categories
- Major Categories (weights approximate as of 2024):
  - Housing (shelter): ~36.2%
  - Food: ~13.5% (food at home ~8.5%, food away ~4.9%)
  - Transportation: ~15.3%
  - Medical care: ~6.8%
  - Energy: ~7.0%
  - Recreation, education, other: ~21.2%
- Core CPI: Excludes food and energy (more stable, Fed-preferred for trend)
- CPI-W: Wage earners only — used for Social Security COLA adjustments
- Chained CPI (C-CPI-U): Accounts for substitution effects, runs ~0.3% lower than headline

#### PCE — Personal Consumption Expenditures Price Index
- Published by: Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA), monthly
- FRED Series: PCEPI (headline), PCEPILFE (core)
- Federal Reserve's **preferred inflation measure**
- Why Fed prefers PCE over CPI:
  - Broader coverage (includes employer-paid healthcare, etc.)
  - Uses chain-weighting (accounts for substitution)
  - Less housing weight (~17% vs 36% in CPI) — less volatile
  - Historically runs ~0.3–0.5% below CPI
- Target: Fed's explicit target is 2% PCE (set formally in January 2012)

#### PPI — Producer Price Index
- Measures prices at the wholesale/producer level
- Leading indicator: PPI rises before CPI as cost pressures pass through
- Final demand PPI is most watched

### Historical Inflation Episodes

| Period | Peak CPI YoY | Cause |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| WWI 1917–1918 | ~20% | War spending, supply disruption |
| Post-WWI 1919–1920 | ~23.7% | Reconstruction demand surge |
| WWII 1942–1947 | ~18–20% | War economy, then supply return |
| Korean War 1951 | ~9.4% | Korean War spending |
| Great Inflation 1965–1982 | Peak 14.8% (1980) | LBJ's "guns and butter," oil shocks 1973 & 1979, loose Fed policy |
| 1973 Oil Shock | CPI ~12% | OPEC embargo, oil price 4x |
| 1979 Oil Shock | CPI ~13.3% | Iranian Revolution, oil supply cut |
| Volcker Disinflation 1980–1983 | FFR peaked 20%, CPI fell from 14.8% to 3.2% | Deliberate Fed tightening |
| Great Moderation 1983–2020 | avg ~2.5% | Globalization, tech, credible Fed |
| COVID Inflation 2021–2023 | Peak 9.1% June 2022 | Supply chain collapse + $5T fiscal stimulus + demand surge |
| 2024 Disinflation | ~3.0–3.5% (sticky) | Fed tightening 2022–2024, supply normalization |

### Hyperinflation Thresholds
- Academic definition: >50% per month (Cagan 1956)
- Practical threshold: >100% annually, currency flight begins
- Notable examples:
  - Germany 1923: 29,500% per month at peak
  - Hungary 1946: 4.19 × 10^16 % per month (worst ever recorded)
  - Zimbabwe 2008: ~79.6 billion % per month
  - Venezuela 2018: ~1,000,000% annually
  - Argentina 2023: ~211% annually
- US has never experienced hyperinflation; closest was post-WWI ~23%

### Fed's 2% Target
- Officially adopted: January 25, 2012 (FOMC statement)
- Rationale: Low enough to avoid distortions, high enough to avoid deflation trap (provides buffer)
- Average inflation targeting (AIT): Adopted August 2020 — Fed allows inflation to run above 2% temporarily after below-target period
- PCE was below 2% for most of 2012–2020 (averaged ~1.5%)
- 2021–2023 period: massive overshoot requiring aggressive tightening

### Inflation Signals for Markets
- Rising inflation → Fed tightens → rising rates → P/E compression → stock headwind
- Falling inflation → Fed eases → lower rates → P/E expansion → stock tailwind
- Inflation > 4%: historically negative for equities in real terms
- Inflation 2–3%: historically sweet spot for equity returns
- Deflation: most dangerous — Japan 1990s shows demand collapse spiral

## 2. GDP — GROSS DOMESTIC PRODUCT

### Definition
GDP = Total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a specific time period.

### Expenditure Formula
**GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)**
- C = Private consumption (households) — ~70% of US GDP
- I = Private investment (business capex, housing, inventories) — ~18%
- G = Government spending (not transfers) — ~17%
- X−M = Net exports (US typically negative, i.e., trade deficit) — roughly −3 to −5%

### Real vs Nominal GDP
- Nominal GDP: Measured in current prices; includes inflation
- Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation using base-year prices (currently 2017 chained dollars)
- GDP Deflator: Nominal/Real × 100 — broader than CPI, covers all of GDP
- FRED Series: GDP (nominal), GDPC1 (real, seasonally adjusted annual rate)

### US GDP Historical Size
- 1929: ~$105 billion nominal
- 1950: ~$300 billion
- 1980: ~$2.9 trillion
- 2000: ~$10.6 trillion
- 2010: ~$15.0 trillion
- 2020: ~$21.0 trillion (COVID dip from $21.7T)
- 2023: ~$27.4 trillion (largest GDP in world)
- 2025: ~$29–30 trillion (estimated)

### Real GDP Growth Rates by Decade (US, annualized avg)
| Decade | Avg Real GDP Growth |
|--------|---------------------|
| 1950s | ~4.2% |
| 1960s | ~4.4% |
| 1970s | ~3.2% (oil shocks) |
| 1980s | ~3.1% (early recession, then boom) |
| 1990s | ~3.2% |
| 2000s | ~1.8% (two recessions) |
| 2010s | ~2.3% (slow recovery) |
| 2020s | ~2.0% est (COVID distortion) |

### Recession Definition
- **NBER (official):** "A significant decline in economic activity that is spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months" — considers GDP, employment, income, sales. NOT just 2 quarters.
- **Technical/popular definition:** Two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth
- NBER Business Cycle Dating Committee officially declares recession start/end dates (often with lag)
- Average US recession since WWII: ~10.4 months
- Average US expansion since WWII: ~58 months

### GDP Signals for Markets
- GDP growth >3%: strong bull market environment
- GDP growth 1–3%: neutral/moderate
- GDP growth <0%: recession risk, bear market pressure
- GDP surprise to upside: USD strengthens, rates rise, stocks mixed
- GDP contraction confirmed: defensive rotation, Fed easing expected

### BEA GDP Revisions (3 rounds)
1. Advance estimate: ~28 days after quarter ends
2. Second estimate: ~56 days
3. Third estimate: ~84 days
Markets move most on advance estimate.

## 3. UNEMPLOYMENT

### Key Measures

#### U-3 — Headline Unemployment Rate
- Official unemployment rate: unemployed persons actively seeking work / labor force
- Labor force = employed + unemployed (actively seeking)
- Does NOT include discouraged workers or part-time-for-economic-reasons
- FRED Series: UNRATE
- BLS publishes monthly in "Employment Situation Summary" (first Friday of month)

#### U-6 — Broad Unemployment Rate
- Includes: U-3 + marginally attached workers + part-time for economic reasons
- Marginally attached: Want work, available, searched in past year but not last 4 weeks
- Discouraged workers: Subset of marginally attached — gave up because no jobs
- U-6 typically runs 4–6 percentage points above U-3
- During 2009 recession: U-3 peaked at 10.0%, U-6 peaked at 17.1%
- FRED Series: U6RATE

#### Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR)
- Civilian labor force / civilian noninstitutional population × 100
- Peak: ~67.3% (2000)
- Post-GFC low: ~62.3% (2015)
- Pre-COVID: ~63.3% (Feb 2020)
- COVID low: ~60.2% (April 2020)
- 2024: ~62.7%
- Long-term trend decline due to Baby Boomer retirements, disability, early retirement
- FRED Series: CIVPART

### NAIRU — Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment
- The unemployment rate consistent with stable (non-accelerating) inflation
- Also called "natural rate of unemployment" or "u*"
- Fed estimates: ~4.0–5.0% historically, currently ~4.0%
- When U-3 < NAIRU: labor market is tight → inflationary pressure
- When U-3 > NAIRU: slack in labor market → deflationary pressure
- During COVID recovery: U-3 fell to 3.4% (Jan 2023), well below NAIRU → contributed to inflation

### Historical Unemployment Peaks
| Event | Peak U-3 | Date |
|-------|----------|------|
| Great Depression | ~25% | 1933 |
| Post-WWII adjustment | ~7.9% | 1949 |
| 1957 recession | ~7.5% | 1958 |
| 1973–74 recession | ~9.0% | 1975 |
| Double-dip 1980–82 | ~10.8% | Dec 1982 |
| 1990–91 recession | ~7.8% | 1992 |
| Dot-com recession | ~6.3% | Jun 2003 |
| Great Recession 2008–09 | ~10.0% | Oct 2009 |
| COVID shock | ~14.7% | Apr 2020 |

### Sahm Rule
- Developed by Claudia Sahm (former Fed economist)
- **Formula:** If the 3-month average U-3 rises 0.5 percentage points above its 12-month low → recession has likely begun
- Historical accuracy: Triggered at or near start of every recession since 1970
- Triggered in July 2024 (0.53pp rise) — created recession debate
- FRED Series: SAHMREALTIME
- Key insight: Unemployment is a lagging indicator, but rate-of-change is more timely

### Jobs Report Key Components
- Nonfarm Payrolls (NFP): Total jobs added/lost in non-farm sector (FRED: PAYEMS)
- Average Hourly Earnings (AHE): Wage inflation proxy — markets watch closely
- Average Weekly Hours: Leading indicator of employment demand
- Birth-Death Model: BLS statistical adjustment adding estimated new business jobs — controversial, can over/undercount at turning points

### Unemployment Signals for Markets
- Unemployment falling: bullish (expansion), but watch wage inflation
- Unemployment rising: bearish, recession signal if paired with other indicators
- Initial jobless claims spike (FRED: ICSA) >400K weekly: recession warning
- NFP miss vs consensus: often triggers immediate equity selloff and bond rally

## 4. INTEREST RATES

### Federal Funds Rate (FFR)
- The overnight rate banks charge each other for reserves
- FOMC sets the target range (currently in 25bp increments)
- FRED Series: FEDFUNDS (effective rate), DFF (daily)
- Actual market rate: SOFR (replaced LIBOR in 2023 for most instruments)

### Historical FFR Key Levels
| Period | FFR Level | Context |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| 1954 | ~1.0% | Post-Korean War |
| 1969 | ~9.2% peak | Vietnam-era inflation |
| 1974 | ~13% peak | Oil shock tightening |
| 1980 | ~20% peak | Volcker shock (highest ever) |
| 1981 | ~19% | Second Volcker hike cycle |
| 1986 | ~6% | Post-Volcker easing |
| 1989 | ~9.8% | Pre-recession peak |
| 1991–93 | 3% | Gulf War recession easing |
| 1995 | 6% | Soft landing cycle |
| 1998 | 5.5% → 4.75% | LTCM/Russia crisis cuts |
| 2001 | 6.5% → 1.75% | Dot-com bust easing |
| 2003–04 | 1.0% (lowest then) | Post dot-com floor |
| 2004–06 | 1% → 5.25% | 425bp tightening cycle |
| 2007–08 | 5.25% → 0–0.25% | GFC emergency cuts |
| 2008–2015 | 0–0.25% (ZLB) | Zero Lower Bound era (7 years) |
| 2015–18 | 0.25% → 2.5% | Gradual normalization |
| 2019 | 2.5% → 1.75% | Insurance cuts (trade war) |
| 2020 | 1.75% → 0–0.25% | COVID emergency cuts |
| 2022–23 | 0.25% → 5.25–5.50% | Fastest tightening since 1980s (525bp in 16 months) |
| 2024–25 | 5.25% → 4.25–4.50% | Gradual easing began Sept 2024 |

### Rate Cycle Lengths (Historical)
- Average tightening cycle: 12–18 months, ~250bp
- Average easing cycle: 12–24 months
- 2022–23 tightening: 16 months, 525bp (historically aggressive)
- Typical lag from first hike to economic effect: 12–18 months (long and variable lags)

### Neutral Rate (r*)
- The theoretical FFR that neither stimulates nor restricts the economy
- Estimated at ~2.5% nominal (0.5% real + 2% inflation target) — pre-COVID Fed consensus
- Post-COVID: Many economists revised r* higher to ~3.0–3.5% due to fiscal expansion
- If FFR > r*: restrictive (contractionary)
- If FFR < r*: accommodative (stimulative)
- Challenge: r* is unobservable, only estimated in real time

### Fed Tools Beyond FFR
- Forward Guidance: Communication about future rate path (manages expectations)
- QE (Quantitative Easing): Fed buys Treasuries/MBS to lower long-term rates
  - QE1: Nov 2008–Mar 2010 ($1.75T)
  - QE2: Nov 2010–Jun 2011 ($600B)
  - QE3: Sep 2012–Oct 2014 ($85B/month, total ~$1.6T)
  - COVID QE: Mar 2020–Mar 2022 ($120B/month, total ~$4.6T added)
- QT (Quantitative Tightening): Fed lets balance sheet shrink by not reinvesting
  - QT1: 2017–2019 (reduced balance sheet from $4.5T to $3.8T before stopping)
  - QT2: 2022–present (reducing from $9T peak)
- IOER/IORB: Interest on reserve balances — sets floor for FFR
- Reverse Repo (RRP): Drains excess liquidity, sets lower bound

### Interest Rate Signals for Markets
- Rate hikes: Negative for growth stocks (duration), mortgage rates rise, housing cools
- Rate cuts: Positive for equities, bonds rally (prices inverse to rates), dollar typically weakens
- Rate pause: Markets often rally in anticipation of cuts
- Inverted yield curve (see below) caused by hikes pushing short end above long end

## 5. YIELD CURVES

### What Is the Yield Curve
A plot of Treasury bond yields across different maturities (from 1-month to 30-year).
Normal curve: Long-term yields > short-term yields (compensates for time/inflation risk)

### Key Spreads Monitored
- **10Y-2Y spread (most watched):** 10-year Treasury minus 2-year Treasury
  - FRED Series: T10Y2Y
  - Normal: Positive (e.g., 10Y at 4%, 2Y at 3% = +100bp spread)
  - Flat: Near 0
  - Inverted: Negative (10Y < 2Y)
- **10Y-3M spread:** 10-year minus 3-month T-bill
  - FRED Series: T10Y3M
  - Some economists prefer this for recession prediction (NY Fed model uses it)

### Yield Curve Shapes and Meaning
| Shape | Condition | Implication |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Normal (steep) | 10Y >> 2Y | Economy expanding, inflation expected, banks profitable |
| Flat | 10Y ≈ 2Y | Transition period, uncertainty |
| Inverted | 10Y < 2Y | Recession signal; Fed policy too tight |
| Bull steepener | Rates fall, long-end faster | Fed cutting, recovery anticipated |
| Bear steepener | Rates rise, long-end faster | Inflation fears, fiscal expansion |
| Bull flattener | Rates fall, short-end faster | Late cycle, easing expected |
| Bear flattener | Rates rise, short-end faster | Fed tightening aggressively |

### Yield Curve Inversions and Recessions (10Y-2Y)
Every US yield curve inversion since 1970 has been followed by a recession:

| Inversion Date | Inversion Depth | Recession Follow | Lag |
|---------------|----------------|-----------------|-----|
| 1973 | −1.0% | 1973–75 recession | ~6 months |
| 1978–80 | −2.5% | 1980 recession | ~12 months |
| 1980–81 | −3.0% | 1981–82 recession | ~6 months |
| 1988–89 | −0.6% | 1990–91 recession | ~12 months |
| 1998 (brief) | −0.2% | (near miss, brief) | No recession immediately |
| 2000 | −0.7% | 2001 recession | ~9 months |
| 2006–07 | −0.6% | 2007–09 recession | ~12–16 months |
| 2019 (brief) | −0.05% | 2020 recession | ~7 months (COVID) |
| 2022–2024 | −1.09% (deepest since 1981) | 2025 recession risk? | Lag ongoing |

- Average lag from first inversion to recession start: ~12–18 months
- The 2022–24 inversion was the longest and deepest in 40+ years
- Duration: July 2022 – September 2024 (over 2 years inverted) — historically unusual

### Why Inversions Predict Recessions
1. Banks borrow short, lend long — inversion squeezes net interest margins → less lending
2. Signals Fed has tightened too aggressively
3. Markets pricing in future rate cuts (lower long-term growth expectations)
4. Self-fulfilling: reduced lending tightens financial conditions

### Yield Curve Signals for Markets
- Steepening from inversion: Often signals recession is coming or already here
- Re-steepening (bear steepener during recession): Early sign of recovery
- 10Y-2Y spread > 150bp: Very bullish for bank stocks
- Spread widening rapidly: Flight to quality (long bonds), risk-off

## 6. RECESSIONS

### NBER Definition
"A significant decline in economic activity that is spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months, normally visible in real GDP, real income, employment, industrial production, and wholesale-retail sales."
- NBER Business Cycle Dating Committee makes official determination
- Often declared 6–12 months after recession has begun (lagging announcement)
- Peak = last month of expansion; Trough = last month of recession

### Post-WWII Recession Statistics
- Number of recessions: 12 (through 2020)
- Average duration: ~10.4 months
- Average GDP decline: ~2.0%
- Shortest: COVID 2020 (2 months)
- Longest: Great Depression (43 months) / GFC (18 months) in modern era
- Average time between recessions: ~58 months (expansion)
- Longest expansion: Jun 2009 – Feb 2020 (128 months, longest in US history)

### Leading Recession Indicators
1. Yield curve inversion (10Y-2Y or 10Y-3M)
2. ISM Manufacturing PMI below 50 (especially for 3+ months)
3. Conference Board Leading Economic Index (LEI) declining 6+ months
4. Sahm Rule trigger (unemployment 3M avg +0.5pp above 12M low)
5. Building permits decline
6. Credit spreads widening (IG and HY)
7. Consumer confidence sharp decline
8. Inverted real yield curve
9. Declining real retail sales

## 7. CREDIT SPREADS

### What Are Credit Spreads
The yield premium investors demand for holding corporate bonds vs comparable-maturity risk-free Treasuries.
Spread = Corporate Bond Yield − Treasury Yield (same maturity)
Expressed in basis points (1bp = 0.01%)

### Key Categories

#### Investment Grade (IG) Spreads
- Bonds rated BBB-/Baa3 or above (Moody's/S&P)
- Typical spread in benign environment: 80–120bp
- Spread during stress: 200–400bp
- FRED Series: BAMLC0A0CM (IG OAS, all maturities)
- ICE BofA US Corporate Index is benchmark

#### High Yield (HY) / Junk Spreads
- Bonds rated BB+/Ba1 or below
- Typical spread in benign environment: 300–400bp
- Spread during stress: 600–1000bp+
- All-time peak: ~2000bp (December 2008, GFC)
- FRED Series: BAMLH0A0HYM2 (HY OAS)

#### OAS — Option-Adjusted Spread
- Spread after removing embedded options (call/put features) from bond pricing
- Most accurate measure of credit premium alone
- Standard metric for comparing bonds with different structures

### Historical Spread Levels

| Period | IG OAS | HY OAS | Market Context |
|--------|--------|--------|----------------|
| 2004–2007 (pre-GFC) | 60–90bp | 250–300bp | Extreme complacency, risk mispriced |
| Oct 2008 (GFC peak) | ~600bp | ~2000bp | Systemic panic |
| 2012 (Euro crisis) | ~230bp | ~700bp | Eurozone sovereign stress |
| 2016 (oil crash) | ~180bp | ~850bp | Energy sector stress |
| Mar 2020 (COVID) | ~374bp | ~1100bp | Pandemic panic |
| 2021–22 (post-COVID) | 80–100bp | 280–320bp | Very tight spreads |
| 2024 | 90–110bp | 300–350bp | Benign, tight vs historical |

### Spread Widening as Leading Indicator
- HY spreads widen 6–9 months before recessions on average
- Spread widening >100bp from cycle lows in HY: Caution signal
- Spread widening >200bp: Strong recession warning
- Spread widening >500bp (IG): Crisis/systemic risk

### Why Spreads Matter
- Funding cost: As spreads widen, corporate borrowing gets more expensive → less capex
- Risk appetite proxy: Tight spreads = confidence; wide spreads = fear
- Bank stress: IG spreads are used in stress tests (Fed scenarios)
- Credit default swaps (CDS): Alternative measure, more real-time

### Spread-Equity Relationship
- HY spreads and S&P 500 have strong inverse correlation (~−0.85)
- When HY spreads spike, equities typically sell off
- When spreads are at cycle tights, equities tend to be near highs
- Divergence (stocks rising, spreads widening) = warning signal

## 8. CONSUMER SPENDING

### Scale and Importance
- PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) = ~70% of US nominal GDP
- Consumer is the backbone of US economic cycle
- Breaking down PCE (2024 approximations):
  - Services: ~69% of PCE (housing, healthcare, financial services, restaurants)
  - Durable goods: ~11% (cars, appliances, furniture)
  - Nondurable goods: ~20% (food, clothing, fuel)

### Key Consumer Indicators

#### Retail Sales (Census Bureau)
- Monthly, published ~2 weeks after month-end
- "Advance Retail Sales" is first release; later revised
- Excludes services (only goods)
- "Control group" retail sales: Excludes autos, gas, building materials, food services — best PCE proxy
- FRED Series: RSAFS (total), RRSFS (real)

#### Consumer Confidence
- **Conference Board Consumer Confidence Index (CCI):**
  - Published monthly, last Tuesday of month
  - Based on ~3,000 household survey
  - Baseline: 1985 = 100
  - Expansion readings: 100+
  - Recession-associated readings: Below 80
  - Two components: Present Situation + Expectations

- **University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment (UMCS):**
  - Published twice monthly (preliminary, final)
  - FRED Series: UMCSENT
  - Smaller sample (~500), faster to catch turning points
  - Reading below 65–70: Associated with recessions

#### Personal Savings Rate
- FRED Series: PSAVERT
- Measures % of disposable income saved
- Post-COVID peak: ~33.8% (April 2020, forced saving)
- Pre-COVID normal: ~7–8%
- 2022–23: Fell below 3.5% as consumers drew down savings
- Low savings rate + high credit card debt = consumer stress signal

### Consumer Debt Levels
- Total household debt (2024): ~$17.7 trillion
  - Mortgage: ~$12.5T
  - Auto loans: ~$1.6T
  - Student loans: ~$1.7T
  - Credit card: ~$1.2T (record high)
- Credit card delinquency rates (90+ days): Rising in 2023–24, approaching GFC levels for lower-income cohorts
- Debt service ratio (DSR): Debt payments / disposable income — FRED: TDSP
- High DSR constrains consumer spending

### Consumer Spending Signals for Markets
- Strong retail sales → confirms growth, potential inflation risk
- Weak retail sales → recession concern, especially if consumer is tapped out
- Rising savings rate → contractionary (Keynesian paradox of thrift)
- Falling savings rate from high level → pent-up demand release (often post-recession)
- Credit card delinquencies rising: Lower-income consumer stress spreading up
