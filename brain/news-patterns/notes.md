# News Intelligence Notes — Market Reaction Research
# Category 6: Historical Market Reactions to News Events
# Last Updated: 2026-06-03

---

## METHODOLOGY NOTE

All data below is compiled from:
- Event studies in peer-reviewed financial literature
- LPL Financial, T. Rowe Price, J.P. Morgan, and State Street institutional research
- CBOE / CME Group historical data
- Federal Reserve FRED data
- Visual Capitalist / Voroni App S&P 500 rate-cut analysis
- Multiple academic studies on corporate event reactions

Historical averages are central tendency estimates. Individual event outcomes vary widely based on macro context, company-specific factors, and the degree to which an event was anticipated. The most important variable in nearly every category is: **how much was already priced in before the announcement.**

## 1. LAYOFFS — LARGE SCALE (5,000+ EMPLOYEES)

### Short-Term Reaction (Day 0 to Day 3)
- Academic research (ScienceDirect 2023): Layoff announcements "significantly negatively impact firm stock returns in the short term (t to t+3)"
- Average abnormal return on announcement day: -1% to -3% for large-cap companies
- Exception to short-term decline: Tech sector layoffs during cost-cutting era (2022-2023) were often welcomed by markets in the short term (+1 to +3%) as investors viewed them as efficiency improvements
- Key driver of direction: **Context of layoff matters more than the layoff itself**
  - Recession-driven layoffs (demand weakness): negative reaction, sustained
  - Restructuring/efficiency layoffs (healthy company cutting costs): neutral to positive
  - Strategic pivot layoffs: mixed, depends on strategic narrative

### Medium-Term Reaction (30-90 Days)
- Claim that large layoffs are positive 3-6 months later: **Not consistently supported** by modern data
- 2026 data (CNBC, May 2026): 56% of AI-related layoff announcements traded in the red from announcement date, average decline ~25%
- Context is paramount: in a bull market, restructuring layoffs can outperform; in a bear market, large layoffs signal deteriorating business

### Long-Term Reaction (6-12 Months)
- No reliable pattern — driven entirely by whether underlying business fundamentals improve
- Companies that layoff and then beat forward earnings estimates: strongly positive
- Companies that layoff and miss subsequent quarters: continued decline

### Sector Differences
- **Technology:** Market initially rewarded layoffs in 2022-2023 (over-hiring correction); this pattern is now less consistent
- **Manufacturing/Industrial:** Layoffs signal demand weakness; typically negative both short and medium term
- **Financial Services:** Mixed; depends on whether it reflects competitive adaptation or distress
- **Retail:** Usually negative; signals consumer demand issues or structural sector change

### Key Variables
- Management guidance accompanying layoff: raised guidance + layoff = positive; reduced guidance + layoff = compounded negative
- Balance sheet strength: companies with strong cash positions viewed as strategic; indebted companies viewed as distressed
- Sector/macro environment: bull market restructuring vs. recession survival layoffs are priced very differently

## 2. FEDERAL RESERVE RATE CUTS

### First Cut in a Cycle — Historical Data (11 Fed Easing Cycles Since 1970)
**Source: Visual Capitalist, RBC Wealth Management, CFA Institute analysis**

- Median S&P 500 return over 12 months after first cut: **+11.9%**
- Average S&P 500 return over 12 months after first cut: **+7.6%** (mean pulled down by recession episodes)
- Range of outcomes: -24% (worst) to +41% (best) over 12 months
- Positive return 12 months after first cut: ~70% of instances
- Year 2 of easing cycle (when recession avoided): S&P 500 historically returns **>16%**

### The Critical Split: Recession vs. No Recession
- **Recession accompanies cuts:** Markets decline significantly (Fed cutting into weakness); avg return near -15% to -20% at worst
- **No recession (insurance cuts):** Markets perform very well; avg +15-20% over 12 months
- Historical pre-recession cuts: 1989, 2001, 2007 — all preceded major bear markets
- Historical non-recession cuts: 1995, 1998, 2019 — followed by strong bull market continuation

### Day 0 Reaction (Announcement Day)
- Average S&P 500 reaction on first cut day: **+0.5% to +1.5%** (typically priced in before announcement)
- "Hawkish cut" (cut + aggressive taper or pause language): negative reaction (-1% to -2%)
- Example: December 2024 Fed cut was interpreted as "hawkish" due to fewer projected future cuts → negative market reaction despite actual rate cut

### Sector Rotation on Rate Cuts
- **Beneficiaries:** Rate-sensitive sectors — Real estate (REITs), Utilities, Financials (steep yield curve), small-caps
- **Neutral:** Technology, Consumer discretionary
- **Relative laggard after cuts:** Defensive/high-dividend if rate cuts accompany economic deterioration

## 3. FEDERAL RESERVE RATE HIKES

### First Hike in a Cycle — Historical Data (Since 1989)
**Source: Northwestern Mutual, Institutional Investor, Dividend.com**

- **During full rate-hike cycles since 1989:**
  - DJIA average gain: ~55%
  - S&P 500 average gain: **~62.9%**
  - Nasdaq average gain: ~102.7%
- Bull markets historically survive and often thrive in rate-hike cycles if underlying economy is strong
- Average time from initial rate hike to start of next bear market: **3.5 years**
- Average time from initial hike to start of recession: **4.1 years**

### Immediate Day 0 / Month 0 Reaction
- Average 1-day S&P 500 return on first hike day: **+0.03%** (effectively flat; priced in)
- Average 1-month return after first hike: **+0.83%**
- Since 1999: S&P 500 always declined over next 3 months after first hike (avg -7%)
- But: S&P 500 was higher 1 year later in 4 of 5 major hiking cycles since 1999

### The Key Insight
Short-term uncertainty resolves: markets often sell off into the first hike, then recover as the "uncertainty premium" is removed and investors see the Fed is hiking because the economy is strong enough to handle it.

### What Crushes Markets vs. What Doesn't
- **Hikes that accompany inflation fight (supply shock):** Negative for both bonds AND equities (2022 was extreme example: -25% S&P in calendar year)
- **Hikes that normalize from emergency levels:** Positive; economy strong, earnings rising, just removing accommodation
- **Aggressive/surprise hikes (+75bps at once):** Negative short-term; markets adjust downward

### Sector Reaction to Hikes
- **Hurt:** Tech/growth (long-duration assets; discount rate rises), REITs, Utilities, bond proxies
- **Helped:** Financials (steeper yield curve initially), Energy, Materials (commodities)
- **Neutral:** Healthcare, Consumer staples

## 4. EARNINGS BEATS

### Large Beat (EPS 10%+ Above Consensus)
**Source: Academic PEAD literature, ProTraderDashboard, HeyGoTrade research**

- Average next-day stock price move: **+3% to +5%** for established S&P 500 names
- High-vol or small-cap stocks: can move +10% to +20% on large beats
- S&P 500 typical quarter: ~75% of companies beat EPS consensus (consensus has been set conservatively)
- **Implication:** Simply beating is insufficient for large moves; must beat BY MORE than typical ~4-5% beat

### Revenue Beat vs. EPS Beat
- Revenue beat > EPS beat in market reaction quality
- EPS beat from cost cuts, tax adjustments, or buybacks = lower quality; smaller price reaction
- EPS beat from revenue growth + margin expansion = highest quality; full price reaction

### Guidance Matters More Than the Beat
- **Beat + Raised Guidance:** Strongest possible combination; avg +5% to +8% next day; PEAD follows for 60 days
- **Beat + Maintained Guidance:** Solid; avg +2% to +4% next day
- **Beat + Lowered Guidance:** Negative reaction despite beat; avg -2% to -5%; market is forward-looking
- **Miss + Raised Guidance:** Can be positive (+1% to +3%) if guidance raise is significant

### Pre-Earnings Rally Risk (Buy Rumor Sell News)
- If stock has rallied >15-20% in 6 weeks before earnings, beat is increasingly priced in
- Post-earnings pullback common even on strong beats when stock was heavily bid up pre-earnings
- Pattern: stock fades 2-5% even on beat/raise when valuation stretched pre-announcement

## 5. EARNINGS MISSES

### Large Miss (EPS 10%+ Below Consensus)
**Source: ProTraderDashboard, Kavout analysis, StableBread research**

- Average next-day stock price move: **-5% to -8%** for established names
- High-vol / small-cap: -10% to -25% for large misses
- Negative drift continues post-announcement (negative PEAD): -3% to -7% additional over 60 days

### Miss + Guidance Cut (Worst Case)
- Average reaction: **-8% to -15%** day of announcement
- Extended PEAD downward: additional -5% to -10% over next 60 trading days
- "Double miss" (miss + guide down): among the most reliably negative events in markets
- Analyst downgrades typically follow within 1-5 business days, adding additional selling pressure

### Forward Guidance Impact Details
- Management providing specifics for revenue shortfall matters:
  - "Macro headwinds, temporary": less negative (-5% to -8%)
  - "Structural issue, competitive pressure": more negative (-10% to -20%)
  - "Revised TAM / demand destruction": catastrophic (-20% to -40% for growth names)

### Miss by Sector Context
- **Cyclical companies (industrials, materials, auto):** miss interpreted as demand signal; sector-wide selloff follows
- **Growth tech companies:** miss severely punished if multiple expansion was baked in; avg -15% to -25%
- **Defensive companies (utilities, staples):** smaller magnitude; avg -2% to -5% on miss
- **Banks/Financials:** NIM guidance and credit quality comments outweigh EPS; complex reaction

## 6. MERGERS & ACQUISITIONS

### Target Company Stock Reaction
**Source: CFI Acquisition Premium data, Wall Street Oasis, Schwert (1996) Markup Pricing, DealRoom research**

- **Average acquisition premium over unaffected share price: 20-30%**
- Range: can be as high as 50% in competitive bidding situations
- High technology sector 2018 avg premium: **36.3%**
- Day of announcement: target stock typically gaps up 20-40% immediately
- Stock-for-stock deals: target may not fully gap to offer price (merger arb risk premium)
- Cash deals: target stock trades within 1-3% of offer price immediately (depending on deal certainty)

### Acquirer Company Stock Reaction
- **Average abnormal return on announcement day: -2% to -3%** for acquirer
- Market often punishes acquirer for overpaying / integration risk / debt taken on
- Large cash deals: acquirer down -3% to -5% (balance sheet concern + overpayment signal)
- All-stock deals: acquirer down -2% to -4% (dilution concern)
- Exception: bolt-on acquisitions in same industry with synergy clarity: flat to slightly positive
- Long-term: majority of acquisitions fail to create value for acquirer shareholders (academic consensus)

### Deal Risk Factors
- **Regulatory risk:** Deals in antitrust-sensitive sectors (tech, telecom, pharma) carry significant spread; target may trade 10-15% below offer price due to break risk
- **Hostile bids:** Target rallies; acquirer more negative (hostile premiums tend to be higher)
- **White knight scenarios:** Multiple bidders → target can trade above initial offer price

### Post-Announcement Drift
- Target stock: typically trades at slight discount to offer (merger arb premium) until deal closes
- Acquirer stock: often drifts lower over 6-12 months if market doubts integration synergies

## 7. PRODUCT LAUNCHES (APPLE, TESLA, MAJOR TECH)

### "Buy the Rumor, Sell the News" Pattern
**Source: Dividend.com analysis of Apple product launch history; Nasdaq expert commentary**

- Pattern: stock rallies in weeks/months preceding major product announcement
- Launch day / announcement day: stock often sells off even if product received well
- Why: all optimism priced in during run-up; announcement = conclusion of the "catalyst"

### Apple (AAPL) Historical Product Launch Behavior
- **Pre-launch run-up:** Typically +5% to +15% in the 30-60 days before major product events
- **Announcement day:** Often flat to -2%; sell-the-news effect
- **Post-launch 30 days:** Depends on sell-through data and reviews; can recover if product beats estimates
- **Pattern failure:** When product is genuinely transformational and beats all expectations → stock continues higher (iPhone (2007), M1 chip transition)

### Tesla (TSLA) Product Events
- Highly volatile; TSLA is driven more by Elon Musk commentary and delivery numbers than specific product launches
- Cybertruck reveal: stock fell on unveiling
- Model 3 production: stock fell on production delays, rose when delivery numbers were strong
- **General pattern:** TSLA traders are event-driven; buy into event, sell on actual news

### General Tech Product Launch Framework
- **Known/expected launch:** Rally pre, sell-the-news on day
- **Surprise launch (not rumored):** Can be positive through announcement day
- **Product FAILURE or disappointing specs:** -5% to -15% same day, extended decline

## 8. FDA APPROVALS AND REJECTIONS (BIOTECH)

### FDA Approval — Average Reaction
**Source: Journal of Finance and Accountancy, aabri.com; Caltech PEAD study; UNI biotech study**

- **Small biotech (single-product companies):** Average abnormal return on approval day: **+30% to +60%**
- Range: +10% to +100%+ depending on market size, competition, and degree of surprise
- **Large pharma (multi-product):** Average reaction: +2% to +8% (approval is one of many revenue streams)
- **Pre-announcement run-up risk:** If approval was widely expected, "sell the news" occurs — stock may decline even on approval
- Event studies: positive mean/median abnormal returns concentrated in 0 to +5 days window

### FDA Rejection (Complete Response Letter — CRL) — Average Reaction
**Source: ResearchGate study; biotech event studies**

- **Small biotech:** Average decline: **-40% to -70%**
- Extreme cases: -80% to -90% for companies with no other pipeline
- **Large pharma:** Average decline: -3% to -8% on rejection
- **Key asymmetry:** Downside magnitude from rejection EXCEEDS upside magnitude from approval (confirmed in academic study)
- Duration: negative reactions tend to persist longer than positive reactions after FDA decisions

### Phase Outcomes (Clinical Trials — Not Final FDA Decision)
- **Phase 3 success:** Average +20% to +40% (large programs); +40% to +80% (small biotechs)
- **Phase 3 failure:** Average -50% to -70%; often catastrophic for single-pipeline companies
- **Phase 2 success:** +10% to +30%
- **Phase 2 failure:** -30% to -50%
- **Phase 1 safety data:** Smaller moves; +5% to +15% success; -15% to -25% failure

### Key Factors Affecting Magnitude
1. Company type (early biotech vs. big pharma)
2. Whether outcome was anticipated (priced in vs. surprise)
3. Disease area (rare disease approvals often larger % moves)
4. Competition in the space (first-to-market vs. crowded market)
5. Pricing/reimbursement potential

## 9. GEOPOLITICAL EVENTS

### Average Initial Reaction
**Source: LPL Financial historical study; J.P. Morgan Private Bank; T. Rowe Price; State Street analysis**

- **Average S&P 500 drawdown after major geopolitical shock: -4.7% to -6%**
- Average time to bottom: **19 days** (about 3 weeks)
- Average time to full recovery: **42 days** (about 6 weeks)
- 12-month return after geopolitical shock: **+11% average** (because underlying economy continues)

### Historical Case Studies
- **Cuban Missile Crisis (1962):** -7% during 13-day standoff; recovered within 2 weeks of de-escalation
- **JFK Assassination (1963):** -3% same day; full recovery in 1 day (one of fastest recoveries)
- **1973 Oil Embargo:** -25% over following months (economic shock; not pure geopolitical)
- **Gulf War (1990):** -15% lead-up; rallied 25%+ after conflict began and ended quickly
- **September 11, 2001:** -12% over the week of reopening; recovered within 1 month
- **Iraq War start (2003):** Initial negative reaction; +28.96% over subsequent 12 months
- **Russia-Ukraine War (2022):** Initial selloff; market had already been declining for other reasons (Fed hike cycle)
- **Iran-Israel escalations (2024):** Brief -3% selloff; recovered within days

### Why Markets Recover Quickly
- Geopolitical events rarely change the underlying earnings trajectory of most companies
- Initial shock is driven by uncertainty premium that dissipates as situation stabilizes
- Historical learning: investors have repeatedly seen "buy the geopolitical dip" work
- Exception: when geopolitical event triggers lasting economic disruption (1973 oil embargo, 1939 WWII start)

### What Doesn't Recover Quickly
- Geopolitical events that cause sustained commodity price spikes → feeding into inflation
- Events that disrupt major trade corridors for extended periods
- Events that cause direct sovereign credit concerns (Turkey, Argentina crises)

## 10. FEDERAL RESERVE SPEECHES (POWELL / FOMC)

### Hawkish Surprise (More Aggressive Than Expected)
**Source: Multiple FOMC event studies; Jackson Hole analysis; CFA Institute**

- **Average same-day S&P 500 reaction to hawkish surprise: -1% to -3%**
- Bond market reacts even more strongly: 10-year yield spikes 5-15bps
- Dollar strengthens 0.5-1.5% vs. major currencies
- Most impacted sectors: Technology, REITs, growth stocks (long-duration assets repriced)
- Example: Jackson Hole 2022 (Powell hawkish pivot): S&P 500 fell -3.4% same day

### Dovish Surprise (Less Aggressive / More Accommodative Than Expected)
- **Average same-day S&P 500 reaction to dovish surprise: +1% to +2%**
- Example: Jackson Hole 2025 (Powell dovish, rate cuts signaled): S&P 500 +1.3% same day, Dow +680 points (+1.5%)
- Growth stocks / Nasdaq typically outperform on dovish surprise
- Dollar weakens; gold strengthens on dovish Fed commentary

### "Hawkish Cut" Phenomenon
- When Fed cuts rates but signals slower future pace of cuts → negative market reaction
- December 2024: Fed cut by 0.25% but signaled fewer 2025 cuts → S&P 500 declined despite technical rate cut
- Market trades future path, not current action

### FOMC Meeting Days vs. Non-Meeting Speeches
- FOMC decision days: larger, more reliable reactions; full press conference allows nuance
- Mid-meeting speeches: smaller reactions; often interpreted through current FOMC framework
- Jackson Hole (August annual symposium): treated as near-policy announcement in importance
- Congressional testimony: moderate impact; more Q&A, less opportunity for clean surprise

### Intraday Pattern on Fed Days
- Market often volatile in the hour before announcement (3pm EDT historically; now 2pm EDT)
- First 30 minutes after announcement: initial kneejerk reaction
- Next 30-60 minutes: Powell press conference begins; reaction often extends or reverses
- End of day: market consolidates at new level based on updated policy interpretation

## 11. ADDITIONAL PATTERNS (SUPPLEMENTARY)

### Dividend Initiations / Increases
- **Dividend initiation:** Average +3% to +5% next day (signals management confidence in cash flow)
- **Dividend increase (>10%):** Average +1% to +3%
- **Dividend cut:** Average -5% to -20%; strongly negative (signals cash flow distress)

### Share Buyback Announcements
- **Large buyback announcement (>5% of float):** Average +2% to +4% next day
- Long-term: most buyback announcements are not fully executed; partial arbitrage away
- Stock-specific: works better for cash-generative value companies than distressed buybacks

### CEO Departure
- **Planned succession:** Neutral to slightly positive
- **Sudden resignation / forced departure:** -3% to -10%; uncertainty premium
- **Departure of founder/visionary CEO:** Often severe; -10% to -30% (Bezos exception: market reacted positively)

### Index Inclusion (S&P 500, Russell 2000 Rebalancing)
- **Added to S&P 500:** Average +4% to +8% on announcement; often fades after inclusion date
- **Removed from S&P 500:** Average -5% to -10%
- Reason: forced buying/selling from index-tracking funds (passive inflow effect)

### Credit Rating Changes
- **Downgrade to junk (below BBB-):** Severe for bonds; equity -5% to -15%
- **Investment grade upgrade:** +2% to +5% equity; significant bond rally
- **Watch negative (precursor to downgrade):** -2% to -5%; advance warning discount

### Short Squeeze Events (GameStop-style)
- Cannot be modeled reliably — driven by social media dynamics and gamma exposure
- Short interest > 30% of float + high borrow rates = setup for potential squeeze
- Trigger: positive news or coordinated buying → cascade covering of shorts
- Magnitude: 100-1000%+ in extreme cases; not investable as systematic pattern
